01

Introduction

What is commercial management & why we need it

What is Commercial Management?

The identification of risk and development of business opportunities — to generate profit from inception to completion of the project.

RICS CM Guidance
FIDIC 1999/2017
NRM 1 & NRM 2
AIQS Cost Manual

Why We Need Commercial Management

1

Effective Cost Control

Implement structured cost control systems

2

Risk Management

Identify, evaluate, mitigate risks

3

Avoid Cost Overrun

Stop leaks before they sink the ship

4

Contractual Issues

Manage variations, claims, EOTs

5

Forecasting

Predict and shape the future

Profit Enhancement Avenues

Early completion · Buying gain · Reduced running cost · Value engineering · Increased productivity — these are the levers a commercial manager pulls to enhance margin without breaking contract.

RICS Competency Levels

Level 3
Advice
Advise senior management or clients on strategic commercial matters
Level 2
Application
Apply commercial management principles to live projects
Level 1
Knowledge
Explain how CM integrates with cost control, valuation, and reporting
APC Tip: At Level 3, assessors expect reasoning — why you took specific commercial decisions and how those decisions affected project profitability or client value.

Commercial Management vs Quantity Surveying

Quantity Surveying

Foundation
Focus: Measurement, cost planning, valuation
Output: BOQ, cost plans, estimates
Concern: Accuracy and compliance
Scope: Pre- and post-contract tasks
VS

Commercial Management

Evolution
Focus: Strategy, risk, profitability
Output: CVR, CTC, performance reports
Concern: Financial performance, decisions
Scope: Entire commercial lifecycle
Reflection: QS is the foundation; CM is the evolution that adds leadership, foresight, and accountability to financial stewardship.

Pre-Contract Phase

When commercial management starts — and what to do first

When Does Commercial Management Start?

At the time of collecting the tender — we need to think about why we are collecting this tender, comparing it with our long-term/short-term business plan.

Good Practice

If management decides not to go for that tender (pricing or other reasons), notify the employer immediately to maintain a good relationship for future opportunities.

Pricing the Tender — Two Methods

Estimator's Tender

Usual method using Method of Measurement (MoM). Based on quantity, labour, material, services, plant, etc.

Operational Tender

Experienced people recognize cost or time using their expertise (know-how). E.g., concrete pour priced by experience.

Best Practice: Before submitting any bid, the estimator's estimation should be compared with the operational tender to identify price variance.

Estimator's Estimation Techniques (NRM1)

Unit Method

Simplest, quickest. Count the number of units accommodated in a building.

FASTEST

Superficial Method

Square meter method. First estimate by QS when initial sketches arrive.

EARLY STAGE

Elemental Cost Plan

By cost consultants using NRM1. Evolves with the design through the project.

PROGRESSIVE

Comparative Cost Plan

Compare market alternatives for each part of the building.

OPTIONS

Approximate Quantities

Most accurate & reliable. Bill items with same dimensions grouped together.

★ MOST ACCURATE

Preliminaries (General Items)

General items required to deliver employer's requirements (not directly to complete the work).

BOQ Loading Strategies

Front Loaded

Higher proportion of costs to early stages. Improves cash flow; protects against client insolvency.

Back Loaded

Higher proportion claimed later. Receives bulk amount toward project end.

Spot Pricing

BOQ item prices reflect actual costs at the time of purchase or execution.

2.9 Tender Submission Components

Every tender package has two parts — technical credibility and commercial proposal.

Technical Submission

  • Trade License & Registration
  • Company profile & organisational chart
  • Execution plan & method statements
  • Construction programme (Gantt + critical path)
  • Key personnel CVs & HSE plan
  • Quality Control procedures
  • Labour & plant histograms
  • Record of past projects

Commercial Submission

  • Covering letter & offer price
  • Form of Tender & Bill of Quantities
  • Schedule of Rates & Daywork Rates
  • Schedule of qualifications / exclusions
  • Price breakdown / summary sheets
Real Example

A contractor submitted a technical proposal emphasising modular construction methods to achieve programme efficiency, supporting its bid for a rapid-build school project in Oman.

2.10 Post-Tender Dealings

After submission, tenderers may be invited for clarification or negotiation. The CM coordinates responses and protects commercial integrity.

Attend post-tender clarification meetings
Provide breakdowns for abnormally high/low rates
Review addenda or revised pricing instructions
Participate in value-engineering discussions
Update risk register for revised scope
Reflection: Successful post-tender negotiation requires technical clarity, commercial transparency, and confidence. Over-discounting to secure an award often leads to future losses.

Real Example

A contractor pricing a deep sewer tunnel in Muscat identified ground-water risk. The team added a 5% contingency and submitted an RFI to clarify the dewatering scope — avoiding dispute post-award.

2.11 Common Pre-Contract Errors to Avoid

Ignoring site factors — failing to attend the tender site visit

Copy-pasting previous rates without validating market conditions

Underestimating preliminaries or indirect costs

Submitting incomplete or non-compliant tender documents

Failing to record tender queries and responses

No documented risk assessment before adjudication

Reflection: Each mistake at the pre-contract stage multiplies its impact after contract award. A structured, recorded tender process ensures auditability and defensibility in dispute situations.

If Tender Submissions Continuously Fail

Do a proper study on internal and external factors and study competitors who won:

  1. Reduce the Overhead (OH) — review allocation
  2. Establish a proper strategy — sector focus, target margin
  3. Check contingency factors and optimize — too conservative?

CVR & CTC Deep Dive

Cost Value Reconciliation & Cost to Complete

The Core Equation

Cost + Profit = Tender Price (Value to the Employer)
When Contractor's actual cost goes up, profit shrinks because the tender price is fixed.

The Contractor's actual cost (Cost) for every month is compared with the money claimed from the Employer (Value) to know the profit. CVR provides a "snapshot" of the financial health of the project at a particular time.

Earned Value Method

Cost & schedule performance in a single framework

EVM at a Glance

A management tool to compare actual work with original budget and original schedule. Focused on Schedule & Cost. Generally monitored weekly.

PV = Planned Value  |  EV = Earned Value  |  AC = Actual Cost
SV = EV − PV  |  CV = EV − AC
SPI = EV / PV  |  CPI = EV / AC

Cash Flow Management

Incoming and outgoing money — the lifeblood of every project

What Is Cash Flow?

Incoming or outgoing of money to/from a company over a given period — it shows when and what amount of money is needed for the project. At the early stage, projects normally face negative cash flow (outgoing exceeds incoming).

Supply Chain Management

Procurement, subcontractors & supply chain finance

Supply Chain Management Defined

A vital part of every project. It is the process of managing parties involving in the supply of services, organisations, logistics, people, activities, information and resources that transform raw materials into a finished product that is fit for its purpose.

CM's Duty:
  1. Ensure proper agreement with relevant party
  2. Administer the contract with attention
  3. Plan at the proper stage of the project
  4. Value the work and pay timely

3 Types of Subcontractors

Domestic

Contractor handles all arrangements (identify, tender invitation, bidding, PQQ, evaluation, award). Contractor still must fulfill contractual requirements with Employer.

Named

Employer provides a list of preferred specialists. Contractor must tender among them only. If past bad experience exists, raise it at tender stage.

Nominated / Novated

Employer nominates a single specialist, or signs a contract themselves and transfers (novates) it to the Contractor.
