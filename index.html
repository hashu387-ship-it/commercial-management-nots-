Introduction

The foundation of commercial management in construction projects

1.1 Purpose of this Guidance Note

This guidance note explains, in practical yet technically sound language, how commercial management functions within a contracting organisation to control cost, value, and risk while safeguarding profitability throughout the life of a construction project.

FIDIC 1999/2017

1.3 Core Objectives of Commercial Management

The ultimate aim is to create and protect value throughout the project life cycle to ensure profitability:

1

Financial Forecasting

Ensuring accurate financial forecasting and control

2

Risk Management

Managing risks and opportunities proactively

3

Transparency

Establishing transparent reporting and governance systems

4

Triple Balance

Balancing cost, quality, and time performance

5

Ethical Behaviour

Promoting fair and ethical commercial behaviour

Real-World Example

On a USD 120M hospital project, the commercial manager maintained weekly cost-to-complete forecasts, reconciling site progress with earned-value metrics. This allowed early identification of cost overruns in structural steel, enabling timely procurement of alternative suppliers and preserving the profit margin.

Reflection: Quantity Surveying is the foundation; Commercial Management is the evolution that adds leadership, foresight, and accountability to financial stewardship.

2.2 Pre-Selection Phase Documents

  • Audited Financial Statements (past 3 years)
  • Bonding capacity (bank references, surety limits)
  • Insurance certificates (employer's liability, third-party)
  • P&L Account, Balance Sheet, Auditor's Statement
  • Company Profile (structure, projects, turnover)
  • CVs of Key Personnel
  • Business Registration
  • Resource capabilities (Plant, Fleet, Labour)
  • HSE Performance Records
  • QA/QC Policies and Procedures
Real-World Example

In an airport expansion project, the commercial manager's submission of three years of audited reports and confirmation of a 20 million OMR bonding limit led to successful inclusion on the tender shortlist.

3.1 Profit Enhancement Strategies

Value Engineering

Identify alternative materials or methods delivering equivalent performance at lower cost

↓ Direct Costs

Procurement Re-negotiation

Seek volume discounts or revised payment terms

↑ Cashflow

Productivity Management

Monitor labour efficiency; introduce incentives

↓ Labour Cost

Waste Reduction

Control wastage of concrete, steel, etc.

↑ Material Yield

Re-sequencing

Optimise construction sequence for resource utilisation

↓ Preliminaries

Commercial Claims

Timely notice and substantiation of variations

↑ Entitlement
Real-World Example

In a hospital project, concrete wastage averaged 7%. By introducing batching-plant monitoring and re-training workers, the wastage reduced to 2%, saving OMR 45,000 and increasing net margin by 0.6%.

3.3 Cost Value Reconciliation (CVR)

CVR measures the project's financial position by comparing earned value against actual cost incurred.

CVR 6-Step Process

1 Calculate earned value/Revenue (based on certified quantities)
2 Record actual costs from the ledger
3 Adjust revenue (MOS, under/over measurement, certification)
4 Compute gross margin = Value – Cost
5 Identify major cost drivers
6 Report key deviations and actions

4.1.2 Risk Categories in Construction

Design Risk

Late IFC drawings, design errors → Delay, rework, additional cost

Procurement Risk

Supplier insolvency, price fluctuation → Cost overrun, delivery delays

Execution Risk

Low productivity, safety issues → Inefficiency, penalties

Contractual Risk

Ambiguities, conflicting clauses → Claims and disputes

Financial Risk

Cashflow shortfall, currency fluctuation → Increased finance cost

Environmental Risk

Regulatory change, force majeure → Extended time, cost recovery

4.9 APC Competency Guidance

Demonstrate understanding of:

  • Commercial processes and contract administration
  • Cost control methods (CVR, CTC, Cash Flow, EVA)
  • Risk management and value engineering principles
  • Procurement and subcontract management frameworks
Sample Question: "Explain how a CVR report helps monitor project performance."

Demonstrate practical implementation on live projects:

  • Preparing monthly cost reports and advising on variances
  • Managing subcontractor payments and procurement
  • Evaluating and substantiating variations and claims
  • Contributing to risk reviews and mitigation actions
Sample Question: "Describe a time when your commercial advice helped mitigate a potential loss."

Demonstrate the ability to provide reasoned, evidence-based commercial recommendations:

  • Advising on strategic commercial decisions
  • Evaluating cost implications of design or programme changes
  • Leading negotiation of major subcontract or variation settlements
  • Advising on final account strategy and close-out
Sample Question: "How did your commercial reporting influence management's decision-making?"

Summary & Key Takeaways

The financial and contractual backbone of construction

Strategic Link

Commercial Management links the strategic decisions of tendering with the operational realities of project delivery.

Profit Protection

From pre-contract bid formulation to post-contract value management, CM defines financial success.

Integrated Approach

A professional CM integrates technical knowledge, business strategy, and ethical conduct.

Commercial Conscience

Acts as the organisation's commercial conscience, ensuring decisions are based on accurate data and risk awareness.

Through structured learning, reflective practice, and adherence to RICS ethical standards, candidates can evolve from quantity surveyors into strategic commercial leaders — professionals who not only measure cost but create value.
